The planet Earth is the third planet from the Sun and the only astronomical object known to harbor life. It was formed over 4.5 billion years ago. The Earth's atmosphere is composed primarily of nitrogen (78 percent) and oxygen (21 percent), which are essential for human survival and carbon-based biological life overall.

Mars is the fourth planet from the Sun and the second-smallest planet in the Solar System. It is frequently referred to as the Red Planet because the iron oxide prevalent on its surface gives it a reddish appearance that is distinctive among the astronomical bodies visible to the naked eye. Mars is a terrestrial planet with a thin atmosphere.

Jupiter is the fifth planet from the Sun and the largest in the Solar System. It is a gas giant with a mass more than two and a half times that of all the other planets in the Solar System combined, but slightly less than one-thousandth the mass of the Sun.

Saturn is the sixth planet from the Sun and the second-largest in the Solar System, after Jupiter. It is a gas giant with an average radius of about nine and a half times that of Earth. It is most famous for its prominent ring system, which is composed mostly of ice particles, with a smaller amount of rocky debris and dust.

Pluto is a dwarf planet in the Kuiper belt, a ring of bodies beyond the orbit of Neptune. It was the first and the largest Kuiper belt object to be discovered. After being classified as a planet for 76 years, Pluto was famously reclassified as a dwarf planet by the International Astronomical Union in 2006.
